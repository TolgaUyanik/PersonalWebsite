1. The 80/20 Rule of Headlines

David Ogilvy said it best: "Once you've written your headline, you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar." Alex takes this further—he spends 80% of his creative time on just the first 5 seconds.

The $100 Million Lesson

A friend of Alex spent $250,000 on an infomercial that flopped. He flew Larry King back out, re-recorded just the first 30 seconds, and the same ad generated $100 million in sales. The only change was the hook.

Hook Creation Process

Every time Alex records ads, he writes 50 hooks:

  • 40 hooks (80%) — Proven winners from past campaigns
  • 10 hooks (20%) — New experimental ideas

The Perfect Hook Formula

How to [DESIRED OUTCOME] without [PAIN POINT] even if [BIGGEST FEAR]

Example: "How to speak confidently in front of strangers without ever practicing even if you're deathly afraid of public speaking"

What Makes a Great Hook

Element Purpose
Curiosity Makes them need to know more
Different Stands out from competitors
Specific Calls out exact audience
Simple Understood in 3 seconds

Pro Tip: Steal Like an Artist

Look at ads working in completely different industries. Can you take a headline from travel and apply it to fitness? From weight loss to B2B? The best hooks are often borrowed from unexpected places.

2. The Call-Out Framework

The goal of every ad is to make someone think: "This is for me." You do that by calling them out specifically—and even more powerfully, by calling out who it's NOT for.

❌ Generic: "Are you a business owner?"

✅ Specific: "If you're a gym owner doing under $50k/month who's tired of inconsistent lead flow..."

The Exclusion Principle

Saying who it's NOT for is more powerful than saying who it's for. When you exclude people, you:

  • Position yourself as premium/selective
  • Make the right customers feel chosen
  • Filter out time-wasters before they contact you
  • Increase perceived value

Exclusion Formula

"If you're [WRONG FIT], this isn't for you. Go consume my free content instead."

Example: "If you're below $500,000/year, this program isn't for you—go read my books first."

Five Levels of Awareness

Your messaging should match where your prospect is in their awareness journey:

Level They Know... Hook Strategy
Unaware Nothing about the problem Pure curiosity
Problem Aware They have a problem "Do you experience [symptom]?"
Solution Aware Solutions exist "Have you tried [solution type]?"
Product Aware Your product category Compare to alternatives
Most Aware You specifically Direct offers

Describe Moments, Not Concepts

Bad: "Are you overweight?"

Good: "Do you have trouble walking upstairs without sweating in places you didn't know you could?"

Specificity creates connection. Describe the moment they experience the problem.

3. The Damaging Admission Technique

This might be Alex's favorite technique. Research shows that when you admit your bias upfront, people are MORE likely to believe you—not less. It's counterintuitive but incredibly powerful.

The Structure

[ADMIT FLAW] but [BENEFIT YOU WANT THEM TO BELIEVE]

Key: What comes AFTER "but" is what they believe. The flaw builds trust for the benefit.

Real Examples

Damaging Admission Benefit
"These markers smell terrible" "but they write 4x longer than others"
"Our gym has terrible parking" "but our sessions are more fun than anyone else's"
"Our websites are ugly as hell" "but they convert like crazy"
"This only works for 4-6 hours" "but you'll have the time of your life"
"If you want to stand out in a crowded marketplace, tell the truth. It makes you more authentic. And if you can do it with humor, you get bonus points."

The Eminem Strategy

In rap battles, Eminem would own all his flaws first: "I'm short, I'm white, I'm [everything else]." His opponents had nothing left to attack. In marketing, when you claim your flaws before prospects think them, you gain trust AND pull the punches from competitors.

Action Step

List every flaw, complaint, and thing you could do better. Then pair each with your strongest benefit using the "but" structure.

4. The Value Equation

When people say "provide value," what does that actually mean? Alex breaks it down into four concrete variables:

The Value Equation

        Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood
VALUE = ────────────────────────────────────────
          Time Delay × Effort Required

The Four Variables

Variable What It Is How to Improve
Dream Outcome What they actually want Promise status, money, health—not features
Perceived Likelihood Belief they'll achieve it Add proof, guarantees, testimonials
Time Delay How long until results Make it faster ("results in 7 days")
Effort Required How hard it is Make it easier ("done-for-you")

Why Liposuction Costs More Than a Diet PDF

Both promise the same outcome (weight loss). But liposuction has:

  • Higher likelihood (surgeon does it for you)
  • Less time (instant results)
  • Less effort (you're asleep)

Same dream outcome, but dramatically higher value on the other three variables.

The Ultimate Offer

Maximum value = something they desperately want + zero risk + instant results + no effort required. The closer you get to this, the more you can charge.

5. Proof & Authority

Proof beats promise. Always. Competitors can copy your offer, but they cannot copy your proof. That has to be earned.

Types of Proof

Type Example
Results "Average customer gains $100k/year"
Volume "Helped 5,000+ businesses"
Longevity "In business for 40 years"
Credentials "Only triple black belt in Ohio"
Social "4.6 stars from 4,000 reviews"
Media "As seen on Forbes"

Proof Presentation Formula

[X%] of [WHO] achieve [OUTCOME] in [TIMEFRAME] under [CONDITIONS]

Example: "The average gym owner makes an extra $100,000 per year in profit after their first 12 months in our program"

The Restaurant Test

Which restaurant do you choose?

  • 5 stars with 3 reviews
  • 4.6 stars with 4,000 reviews

You pick the 4.6. Volume of proof creates implied authority that perfect scores with tiny samples never can.

Say What Only You Can Say

AI can lie, but it can never have DONE something. Your experiences, your results, your story—these are uncopiable. Document everything. Track all customer results. This is your moat.

6. The More → Better → New Framework

When you're trying to scale, there's a specific order of operations:

The Scaling Sequence

1. MORE → Do 10x the volume
     ↓
2. BETTER → Optimize when ROI of better > ROI of more
     ↓
3. NEW → Only when current channels maxed

The Volume Reality Check

Alex paid a consultant who had a much bigger brand. The consultant looked at Alex's content output and said:

"Dude, you don't need to make your content better. You're just not making anything. I post 5x a day. You haven't even posted."

The people ahead of you aren't doing 2x more—they're often doing 10x to 100x more.

When to Shift to "Better"

Only when the return on effort for optimizing exceeds the return on one more unit of volume. Going from 1 salesperson to 2 might double sales. Going from 10 to 11 only adds 10%. At that point, optimizing the existing 10 might yield better returns.

The 10X Question

"What would break first if we 10x'd the business tomorrow?"

Identify it. Fix it. Then scale. Find the next bottleneck. Repeat.

7. LTV to CAC: The Only Metric That Matters

If Alex could only know ONE metric about a business, it would be the LTV to CAC ratio.

The Formula

LTV (Lifetime Gross Profit) ÷ CAC (Cost to Acquire Customer) = Your Ratio

Benchmarks

Ratio What It Means
3:1 Minimum viable
5:1 Healthy business
10:1 Strong—ready to scale
30:1+ Scale aggressively NOW

Starbucks Makes $14,000 Per Customer

That's their lifetime value. Even if customer acquisition cost $500, they'd still be getting 28:1 returns. This is why they can afford prime real estate everywhere. Your LTV determines how aggressively you can acquire customers.

The 30-Day Payback Rule

Alex aims to get his CAC back within 30 days. If you sell something for $10/month and people stay for 10 months ($100 LTV), consider a one-time upcharge upfront to offset acquisition cost. Then the rest of the relationship is pure profit.

8. Copy That Converts

Third-Grade Reading Level

Most copy doesn't convert because people have to pause to understand it. The moment they pause, they stop.

Assume Your Reader:

  • Has no idea who you are
  • Is in a rush
  • Doesn't care about your thing
  • Has a third-grade reading level
  • Is distracted

Writing Rules

  • Short sentences. Small words. Big promises.
  • Fewer adverbs, better verbs. "He sprinted" beats "He ran quickly"
  • Delete anything that makes them pause.
  • Test everything through a reading level calculator until it hits 3rd grade.

The PS Statement

The two most-read parts of any piece of copy:

  1. The headline
  2. The PS statement

Use your PS for:

  • Exclusion: "PS: If you're under $500k/year, this isn't for you"
  • Recap: "PS: We've helped 1,000 people. If you're serious, click here"
  • Reward: Build habit by adding value, then occasionally use for CTAs

Clear CTAs

Make it stupid simple:

"Click the button below. On the next page, fill in your information. Then select a convenient time. You'll receive a confirmation email."

Tell them what to do AND what will happen. When it happens exactly as you said, you build trust for the next instruction.

9. The Power of Niching Down

"Better creates bigger. Bigger creates bloat."

Most businesses expand to grow. The best businesses get BETTER at serving their niche, and growth follows naturally.

The $7 Million Instagram Account

Alex met someone making $7 million per year from just 5,800 Instagram followers. Her entire account was about one thing: how registered dietitians can bill insurance more effectively.

Niche of a niche of a niche. But 5,700 of those 5,800 followers were her exact target customer.

The Vista PE Method

Vista Private Equity (100+ billion AUM) shared their secret:

  1. List all customers by profit
  2. Identify the top 20%
  3. Find what they have in common
  4. Only sell to those people going forward
  5. Phase out the bottom 80%

Same size business, 5x the revenue, way higher margins.

Gym Launch: A Niche Case Study

People think Gym Launch serves all gyms. Reality:

  • No personal trainers
  • No big box facilities
  • No yoga, spin, or Pilates studios
  • Only micro gyms doing before/after transformations

That business still does $30+ million per year.
