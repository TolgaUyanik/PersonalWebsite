1. Sales Multipliers (Before You Even Talk)

These are the things that happen outside the sale that affect your numbers more than anything you say on the call. Master these first.

+29%

Sell 7 Days Per Week

52 weeks × 2 days = 104 extra sales days per year. Weekend prospects are often more available and less stressed than weekday callers.

+391%

Respond in Under 1 Minute

Contacting leads within 60 seconds increases close likelihood by 391%. People have huge motivation for tiny periods—open the door when they knock.

-80%

The 5-Minute Drop-Off

If you wait longer than 5 minutes, close likelihood drops by 80%. Four out of five deals you would have won are now gone.

50%

First Responder Wins

Half of all prospects go with the business that responds first—not the best one. Speed beats perfection.

The Nail Salon Lesson

When Leila needed her nails done in a new city, she called the top-rated salon. "We're appointment only—can book you in two days." She hung up. Called the next one. Same thing. Third one said "We can take you right now." They got the business—and probably her repeat business forever. The first two lost money by not being available.

Feed the Killers

One of Alex's most counterintuitive strategies: Best leads go to best closers. Worst leads go to worst closers.

The Time Share Story

The #1 sales rep at a multi-billion dollar timeshare company (out of 3,000 reps) won the sales competition and got one hour with the CEO. His pitch: "Stop giving me garbage leads and giving qualified prospects to new reps. Give me the best leads—I'll close them. Give new reps the hard ones to learn on."

Result: His income went from $250,000/year to $3 million/year. When rolled out company-wide, it 5x'd the entire business.

The Manual Reminder Sequence

When What to Do
24 hours before Voice/video: "Did some prep for you, found 3 things we can help with immediately. Excited to share tomorrow."
Morning of Text: "Looking forward to our call today at [time]"
1 hour before Text: "See you in 60 minutes, let me know if any issues getting on"

Pro tip: Show that you've done prep work. "I set aside this t-shirt for you—what color?" or "I reviewed your site with my team, found 3 quick wins." This makes them feel obligated to show up.

2. Maximizing Opportunities

The guy who works the most hours sells the most deals. Every single time. But it's not just about hours—it's about what you do with them.

Pull Up Appointments

If someone books for Thursday and you have an opening today—call them and pull it up. Same-day appointments have dramatically higher show rates, and you free up Thursday for someone else.

You: "Hey, I had a cancellation this afternoon. Would you be able to come in at 3 instead of waiting until Thursday?"

The Solar Sales Rep

One rep was outselling everyone 4:1. His secret: whenever a new appointment booked, he immediately called to "qualify" them. If they had time right then, he closed them on the spot. If not, he pulled them up to an earlier slot. Either way, he maximized his time on actual sales calls.

The Two SOPs

Most companies only have an "on-the-call" process. The best salespeople also have an "off-the-call" SOP:

On-Call SOP Off-Call SOP
Follow the script Pull up future appointments
Take notes Push unresponsive leads, open slots
Handle objections Follow up with no-shows
Close or BAMFAM Work the kill list
Update CRM Ask recent closes for referrals

BAMFAM: Book A Meeting From A Meeting

Never end a call without knowing when you'll talk next. If they say "let's circle back," address it right then:

Prospect: "Let's connect offline and find another time."

You: "Totally—we've both got our calendars up right now. What does next Tuesday look like for you?"

Ask for Referrals

The Referral Script

"Who do you know who's as awesome as you, who would also benefit from this?"

Notice: "Who do you know" forces a name, not a yes/no. The compliment ("as awesome as you") makes them want to answer.

The 50% Boost

One new sales rep was outperforming the #2 guy by 50%. His secret? He asked every single person for referrals. Even if only 1 in 4 referred, those referrals closed at 80-90%. One sale became two. Customer acquisition cost cut in half.

3. The Power of Preparation

Five minutes of research makes you look like a genius. Everyone else shows up not knowing which way is Sunday.

The 10% Rule

Prep Time = 10% of Call Time

60-minute call = 6 minutes of prep. One-time quarterly meeting = flip it (6 hours prep for 1 hour presentation).

What to Research

  • Their LinkedIn, website, social profiles
  • Recent news about their company
  • Their industry's pain points
  • All notes from previous calls (never make them repeat themselves)

You: "So Mr. Johnson, I see you work at [Company]—looks like you just launched that new product line. How's that going?"

Immediate respect. Check mark. "This person did their homework."

The Gym Owner Story

When Alex started selling to gym owners, he'd piece together profiles from their social media, look at their gym layouts in photos, and come to the call with specific suggestions: "I noticed your floor plan—if you moved the equipment here, you could double usable space for group sessions." They were blown away. He didn't have a reputation yet, so he built it through preparation.

Take Notes → Pass Notes

The best salespeople take detailed notes, then those notes get passed to customer success. The customer never has to repeat their story. Repeating = negative experience. You repeating it back to them = exceptionally positive.

You: "So just reading my notes from our last call—you've got daughters Sarah and Jessica in Colorado, and the main issue has been [X]. Is that still accurate?"

Immediate trust. "This person actually listened."

4. The CLOSER Framework

This is the structure of the sale itself. It's an acronym that's easy to remember and deploy.

Letter Step What You're Doing
C Clarify Why are they here? What problem are they trying to solve?
L Label Confirm the gap: "You're at X, want Y, and Z has been the obstacle."
O Overview Pain Walk through what they've tried, what failed, and why.
S Sell the Vacation Paint the destination, not the plane ride. Your 3-pillar pitch.
E Explain Concerns Handle objections with the AAA framework.
R Reinforce When they say yes, SHUT UP and collect payment.

Clarify: Opening the Call

You: "What made you reach out to us?"

You: "What made you take the time to hop on this call today?"

You: "What made you opt in on the site?"

You're reminding them of actions they took. They showed interest. They have a problem. Get them to articulate it.

Label: Establish the Gap

You: "So just to make sure I'm clear—you're currently at [current state], you want to get to [desired state], and [obstacle] has been what's holding you back. Is that right?"

Now you've set the table: here's where they are, here's where they want to be, here's the dragon. You're offering the sword.

Sell the Vacation (3-Pillar Pitch)

Talk about Maui, not TSA, bumpy flights, and bad airplane food. Talk about the transformation, not the process.

The Stool Analogy

Whatever you're selling, chunk it into 3 things. For weight loss: Fitness + Nutrition + Accountability. "It's like a stool—two legs and it falls over. You had nutrition and accountability with Weight Watchers, but no fitness. That's why you gained it back. We give you all three."

Your 3-pillar pitch should take 2 minutes max. You should be talking about the prospect's pain, not your product features.

5. The Pain Cycle

"The pain is the pitch. Deprivation creates motivation."

BF Skinner said you can't make a horse drink. Alex disagrees: "If I dehydrated the horse, bled it out, salted its mouth, and put water in front of it on a hot day—I can guarantee it will drink."

Your job is to expand their awareness of the deprivation so motivation increases.

The Pain Cycle Questions

You: "What have you tried so far to solve this?"

Prospect: "I tried Weight Watchers..."

You: "What did you like about it?"

Prospect: "The accountability was nice..."

You: "What didn't work?"

Prospect: "I gained it all back..."

You: "So you had nutrition and accountability, but no fitness component. That's why the weight came back—you lost muscle. We fix that one thing, and you're set."

Quantify the Pain

  • How much time have they lost?
  • How much money have they spent?
  • What's the daily cost of NOT solving this?
  • How has this affected their status/relationships?

Everything They've Tried Is Your Advantage

They're on the phone with you, which means everything else failed. If someone says "I already have a personal trainer," you can say: "Yeah... he's obviously not doing a good job. You're here."

6. Objection Handling Mastery

Closing begins when someone says no. If everyone immediately said yes, you wouldn't be needed—just put up a video and a buy button.

Kill Zombies Early

An obstacle is a concern that comes up BEFORE you mention price. An objection comes up AFTER. Obstacles are way easier to handle. Address concerns early—don't let zombies get close enough to bite.

First Response Questions

When they say "I need to think about it," don't panic. Ask:

Situation What to Ask
First "no" "What's your main concern?"
Vague stalling "What are you afraid of happening?"
Still stuck "What are the variables you're using to make this decision?"
Total block "What would make this a yes?" / "What would make this a no?"

The AAA Framework

Acknowledge → Associate → Ask

Acknowledge: Repeat back what they said (builds rapport, buys you time)

Associate: Connect to something positive or someone similar who succeeded

Ask: Move forward with the next question

Prospect: "I need to talk to my spouse first."

You: "Totally understand—you want to make sure you're both on the same page. [Acknowledge] Actually, I just spoke with someone this morning who said the exact same thing. Can I share what helped her? [Associate] ... So let me ask—if your spouse said no, what would you do? [Ask]"

Specific Objection Handles

"I need to think about it"

You: "Totally fair. What specifically do you need to think about?"

You: "Do you think the reason you're on this call today is because you've struggled to make this decision in the past?"

"I need to talk to my spouse"

You: "Let's play it out. If they say no... what happens? You keep doing what you've been doing? Five years from now, 25 pounds heavier, still asking permission?"

You: "Are you looking for permission or support? Because those are very different things."

"I don't have the money"

You: "Is it that you don't have it, or that you're not sure it's worth it?"

You: "Have you ever bought something before that you weren't sure you could afford? How'd that work out? You're still here, not on a couch."

"I don't have time"

You: "Is this a seasonal thing, or is it always like this?"

You: "If it's always busy, shouldn't you start now—when you have the most support?"

The Best Case / Worst Case Close

You: "Let's play it out. Best case: you change your life forever. Worst case: you're in the same position you've been in before—and you were fine then too. Does that feel like a bet worth taking?"

The Dave Ramsey Lady

A prospect said she followed Dave Ramsey and the payment would be her grocery envelope for the week. Alex asked what she was afraid of. "Not being able to afford food." His response: "But you're trying to lose weight. Worst case you can't afford food and lose some weight. Win-win." She laughed—and he closed her.

7. The Art of Asking

The Law of Closing

He who asks the most times gets the most sales.

But here's the catch: if you ask wrong, you lose rapport and can't ask again. The key is maintaining rapport so you get unlimited shots on goal.

Details Are Death Traps

When prospects ask detail questions, they're trying to find something to nitpick. Never answer blind.

They Ask You Ask Back
"What days are you open?" "What days were you looking for?"
"What's included?" "What were you hoping would be included?"
"What certifications do you have?" "Which certifications are you looking for?"

The Mechanic Close

If they press hard on details you can't answer:

You: "I can't ethically answer that until we look under the hood. It's like asking a mechanic what's wrong with your car before you bring it in. Anyone who gives you an answer without understanding your situation first—run the other way."

Be Like Smoke

Questions can't be disagreed with. Statements can. When you answer with a question, they can't attack you.

Prospect: "What makes you better than your competition?"

You: "What things are you looking for?"

Prospect: "I want X, Y, and Z."

You: "Why are those important to you?"

Hot potato—right back to them. You're like smoke. They can't catch you.

They Believe Nothing You Say

They believe everything THEY say. That's why you ask questions—you need them to say it's a good idea, not you.

Childlike Curiosity

When they say no, your instinct shouldn't be to hard-close. It should be genuine curiosity:

You: "Huh. That's interesting. I wouldn't have expected that. Can you tell me more about that?"

Head tilted up. Genuinely curious. Not competitive. This keeps rapport intact.

8. Consistency & Meta-Skills

Maximum opportunities + maximum conversion rate = nothing if you can only do it for a day. The difference between champions and everyone else: sustained performance.

Dogs, Horses, and Tigers

Dogs: Need to be fed, need belly rubs, die without constant attention

Horses: Gallop when you whip, trot when you stop

Tigers: Can eat a full meal, but if a bunny walks by, they'll kill it for sport

You want tigers. They love the thrill of the hunt. They'd do this for free.

Kill for Sport

Every "bad" lead is free practice with stakes. When Alex sold weight loss, homeless people would sometimes respond to his ads. Two options:

  • Just get through it and move on
  • See it as free practice and close them anyway

The number of times "poor-looking" prospects turned out to have money was more than he could count. And even if they don't buy, you just sharpened your sword.

Track Data Religiously

The skill of any person in any endeavor is judged by the quantity and quality of metrics they track.

Metric Why It Matters
Schedule Rate Are you booking appointments?
Show Rate Are they actually showing up?
Offer Rate Are you getting to the pitch?
Close Rate Are you closing?
Cash Collected Are you getting paid?
Calls to Close One-call? Two-call? Average?

Key insight: Increasing show rate by 20% is just as valuable as increasing close rate by 20%—and usually way easier.

Never Blame Circumstances

"The leads are fine. I need to get better."
"I take 100% control of outcomes."
"The worse the lead, the more I flex my skill."

When You're in a Slump

  1. Record yourself when hot — Analyze what you do differently when you're closing everything
  2. Read testimonials — Rebuild conviction in what you're selling
  3. Drill one thing — Don't try to fix everything at once
  4. Jump on a mini trampoline — You literally cannot frown while jumping (Alex used this between sessions when doing 25+ sales per day)

Follow the Script

If your team doesn't follow the script, there's no point in having one. Script adherence comes first. The script should include:

  • Words — What to say
  • Emphasis — Which words to stress
  • Tone — When to go up/down
  • Pacing — When to slow down for importance

Same Words, Different Meaning

"I didn't say he hit his wife" has 7 different meanings depending on which word you emphasize. The script isn't just words—it's how you deliver them.

When They Say Yes

SHUT UP

The moment you have agreement, stop talking. Ask for payment info. Close. Do not bring up new features. Do not add details. Do not sell yourself out of a sale.

The Core Philosophy

"Sales is an education process over a bridge of trust. If the prospect knew everything you knew, they would buy. Your job is to fill in the fewest gaps needed for them to make the decision."
"You want to be right, or you want to be rich? You become rich by making THEM right."
"Whoever cares most about the prospect wins the sale."
